**Section 1: "The Risk Nobody Hedges"**
- Opening line EXACTLY: "There's a latent risk that discourages passive liquidity providers — and no current hedging instrument tracks it on-chain."
- Define adverse competition in plain English: sophisticated LPs (JIT, MEV-aware) concentrate fees away from passive LPs. This is not healthy competition — Capponi, Jia & Zhu (2024) show JIT substitutes passive LP activity rather than complementing it, which decreases overall market quality.
- State clearly: this is NOT impermanent loss, NOT LVR, NOT price risk. It is fee concentration risk — a distinct, unhedged dimension.

**Section 2: "A Passive LP's Experience"**
- Walk through one LP's journey in ETH/USDC 30bps pool:
  1. LP deposits liquidity. Fair fee share = 1/N of pool fees.
  2. Sophisticated LP (JIT) enters around a large trade. Fee share drops from 1/N to $x_k \ll 1/N$.
  3. Effective fee rate drops. Per Capponi & Zhu (2024), lower effective fee rate means the LP's optimal exit threshold is reached sooner.
  4. LP exits earlier than they would in a fair-share world.
- Use `$...$` for inline math (GitHub renders it).

**Section 3: "Two Properties That Make This Hedgeable"**
1. **Path-dependent tail risk**: Not every day has adverse concentration. Our data shows 63% of days have positive deviation ($\Delta^+ > 0$), but only 1 day in 41 exceeds the critical turning point $\delta^* \approx 0.09$. Risk is rare but severe — classic insurable tail.
2. **Orthogonal to IL/LVR**: Impermanent loss tracks price divergence. LVR tracks MEV extraction. Fee concentration tracks competition structure. The econometric model includes an IL control variable; it does NOT absorb the concentration effect. These risks can be hedged independently.

**Section 4: "What the Literature Says"**
Five papers, each with a bold name, full citation, and one-sentence contribution:
- **Capponi, Jia & Zhu (2024)**: JIT liquidity crowds out passive LPs by substituting their fee share, not complementing it
- **Capponi & Zhu (2024)**: LPs have optimal exit thresholds; lower effective fee rate means earlier exit
- **Ma & Crapis (2024)**: Equal fee share (1/N) is the competitive equilibrium baseline — deviation from this is the treatment variable
- **Aquilina et al. (2024, BIS)**: 7% of LP addresses capture 80% of fees empirically
- **Bichuch & Feinstein (2024)**: LP fee rate is priceable as a derivative; their fixed-for-floating fee swap is structurally identical to ThetaSwap's design

**Section 5: "Key Statistics"**
Markdown table with columns: Statistic | Value | Source. Include all values from the Key Facts header block plus: trigger days (1 in 41), turning point ($\delta^* \approx 0.09$).

**Introduction paragraph**: One paragraph framing the research approach — quadratic deviation exit hazard model applied to 3,365 position-day observations from ETH/USDC 30bps (41 days, 600 positions, 597 exit events). Three pillars organize the findings.

**Pillar 1: "Demand Exists — The Inverted-U"**

- Plain English lead: "Passive LPs respond to fee concentration in a non-linear way. Below a threshold, concentration is actually protective. Above it, LPs flee."
- The model (with plain-English annotation before AND after the equation):
  "The probability that a position exits on day $t$ depends on how concentrated fees are ($\Delta^+$), the square of that concentration (capturing non-linearity), impermanent loss as a control, and position age:"

  $$P(\text{exit}) = \sigma(\beta_0 + \beta_1 \Delta^+ + \beta_2 (\Delta^+)^2 + \beta_3 \cdot \text{IL} + \beta_4 \cdot \log(\text{age}))$$

  "Translation: the logistic model says exits depend on concentration deviation and its square. The negative $\beta_1$ and positive $\beta_2$ create an inverted-U — exactly what Capponi's theory predicts."

- Coefficient table (markdown):

| Coefficient | Lag 1 | Lag 2 | Lag 3 |
|-------------|-------|-------|-------|
| $\beta_1$ (linear) | -23.18 (p=0.012) | -20.45 (p=0.021) | -18.92 (p=0.033) |
| $\beta_2$ (quadratic) | +129.20 (p=0.030) | +112.87 (p=0.045) | +104.33 (p=0.058) |

(Note to executor: Extract exact lag 2 and lag 3 values from econometrics.tex Section 5.7 Table. The values above are from RESEARCH.md for lag 1; verify lag 2-3 against the source.)

- Turning point formula: $\delta^* = -\beta_1 / (2\beta_2) \approx 0.090$
- Plain English: "Below 0.09, concentration actually reduces exits (shelter regime). Above 0.09, it accelerates exits (Capponi regime)."
- Signal dissipates at lags 5-7, confirming short-memory PLP reaction.

**Pillar 2: "A Price Exists — Marginal Effect and Implied Premium"**

- Marginal effect formula: $\frac{dP}{d\Delta} = (\beta_1 + 2\beta_2 \Delta) \cdot \bar{P}(1 - \bar{P})$
- At $\Delta = 0.15$: marginal effect = 2.27, meaning +0.01 in $\Delta^+$ raises exit probability by ~2.3 percentage points.
- Insurance pricing translation: 2.3pp multiplied by 48 hours remaining multiplied by \$100/hr opportunity cost = \$110 implied premium per position.
- Plain English: "This is the maximum a rational LP would pay to avoid concentration risk above the turning point."

**Pillar 3: "Backtest Validates — The p-Squared Payoff"**

- Payoff formula: $\text{payout} = \gamma \cdot \left(\left(\frac{p_{\max}}{p^*}\right)^2 - 1\right)^+$
- Where $p = \Delta^+/(1 - \Delta^+)$ maps deviation to price space, $p^* = \delta^*/(1-\delta^*)$, and $\gamma$ is the base premium factor.
- Results at $R_0 = \$200\text{K}$, $\gamma = 3.30\%$:
  - 18.8% of positions better off hedged (vs 4.7% for trigger-based INS-05)
  - Mean hedge value: +\$23.21 (vs -\$82.65 for INS-05)
- No moral hazard: staying in pool during spikes increases payout (correct incentive alignment).
- Positive correlation between position lifetime and hedge value.
