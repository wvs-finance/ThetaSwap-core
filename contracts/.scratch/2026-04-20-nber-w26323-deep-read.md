## 1. What is the paper about? (≤200 words)

Dew-Becker, Giglio, and Kelly (DGK) price the risk of macroeconomic uncertainty and realized volatility by constructing hedging portfolios of zero-delta straddles across 19 CME futures underlyings (S&P 500, Treasuries, four developed-market currencies — GBP, JPY, CHF, and one other — metals, energies, agricultural products) over 1983–2015. They formally distinguish *uncertainty* σ²ₜ = varₜ₋₁(εₜ) — the forward-looking conditional variance — from *realized volatility* ε²ₜ — the backward-looking squared realization (§1.1, lines 285–310). Short-maturity straddles load primarily on realized squared returns; long-maturity straddles load on implied-volatility innovations. Differencing across maturities therefore yields an "rv portfolio" and an "iv portfolio" for each market, which are then averaged/projected onto the Jurado-Ludvigson-Ng (JLN, 2015) and Baker-Bloom-Davis EPU uncertainty indexes.

Headline finding (§7, lines 1167–1214): portfolios that hedge *uncertainty* earn zero or mildly positive Sharpe ratios (good-uncertainty story), while portfolios that hedge *realized volatility* earn strongly negative Sharpe ratios — up to −1.19 for the S&P 500 rv portfolio, i.e. twice the equity-risk premium. The asymmetry is interpreted through negatively-skewed fundamental shocks: the tail events that *generate* realized volatility are the ones investors most dislike.

## 2. Methodology signatures relevant to our exercise

### Identification strategy
Not IV, not event-study, not panel-FE, not DID. DGK use a **long-short portfolio construction** grounded in the Black-Scholes (1976 futures variant) sensitivities of straddles across maturities: ∂(straddle)/∂(ε²) scales as (2n)⁻¹ for maturity n, while ∂/∂(implied vol) scales differently, so linear combinations of short- and long-maturity straddles mechanically isolate rv vs. iv exposure (§3.1, lines 510–602). Identification is "structural" in the Black-Scholes sense, not in the Hausman/exclusion-restriction sense. The authors also estimate a linear-factor cross-check (§5.4, lines 916–1002) regressing straddle returns on (fᵢₜ/IVᵢₜ₋₁), (fᵢₜ/IVᵢₜ₋₁)², and ΔIVᵢₜ/IVᵢₜ₋₁, which is more robust but less precise.

### Frequency / sample window overlap with our panel
- **DGK**: 1983–2015 (18–31 years per contract), two-week-overlap returns from daily data; monthly realized volatility for correlation tables.
- **Abrigo**: 2008–2026, weekly Friday-anchored 947-obs TRM panel, monthly BanRep remittance aggregate LOCF-interpolated to weekly.

**Overlap**: Only the 2008–2015 window overlaps, and even there no EM-FX or Colombian series appear in DGK's data.

### Use of remittance data, Colombian macro data, or EM FX-vol literature
**None.** Word-level search confirms: zero hits for "remittance*", zero for "Colombia*", zero for "emerging market*" (the single "exchange rate" hits — lines 152 and 1323 — are citations to Darby et al. 1999 on the *effect* of exchange-rate uncertainty on investment, not on its *determinants*). The "currencies" covered are GBP, JPY, CHF (lines 792, 1459–1465, 1492–1498) — developed markets only. There is no EM FX literature review and no remittance-literature reference.

### Econometric choices that DO map onto our spec
1. **Innovation vs. level distinction, and the pitfall of AR residuals on smoothed series** (footnote 23, lines 676–680): DGK explicitly reject using residuals from an AR model on the JLN indexes because JLN is built from a *two-sided filter / smoother*, so "the changes from period to period (or residuals from an autoregressive model) do not represent statistical innovations and are in fact extremely smooth." This is directly applicable to our AR(1) residual construction on BanRep monthly remittance inflows.
2. **Overlapping-returns inference** (§4 footnote 24, lines 735–738; §A bootstrap): "50-day block bootstrap with 5000 replications"; "Hansen–Hodrick type standard errors are not feasible here due to…" (line 738, truncated). GMM standard errors with Hansen-Hodrick are used for the factor-model cross-check (line 1450).
3. **Distinction σ² vs ε² vs. "a quadratic function of price movements"** (§1.1, lines 300–306): both *uncertainty increases and decreases* induce higher realized volatility.
4. **Random-effects estimator for category means across underlyings** (line 997): analogous to how we could aggregate the 13 sensitivity rows if we wanted a summary statistic.
5. **No GARCH as driver — explicit rejection** (footnote 12, lines 317–318): "implied volatility is not a deterministic function of past realized volatility," so a pure GARCH(1,1) cannot reproduce the observed implied/realized correlation structure (0.2 on average). This is a caution — not a contradiction — for our GARCH(1,1)-X co-primary.

## 3. How the paper can help the Abrigo exercise — actionable

### Action 3a (STRONG): Add footnote to Rev-1 §7 (surprise construction) defending our AR(1) choice

DGK (footnote 23, lines 676–680) articulate exactly why our AR(1)-on-BanRep-aggregate approach is *defensible where a two-sided-filter approach would not be*. Our remittance series is an administrative flow series, not a two-sided smoother like JLN; therefore AR(1) residuals ARE valid statistical innovations. We should cite this footnote as affirmative support — currently our spec does not cite any identification-theoretic defense of AR(1). Recommend adding to Rev-1 §7 or §13: "See Dew-Becker, Giglio, and Kelly (2019, NBER WP 26323, fn. 23) for the symmetric caution that AR residuals are *not* statistical innovations when the underlying series is a two-sided filter — a condition that does not apply to BanRep administrative aggregates."

### Action 3b (MODERATE): Strengthen HAC/bootstrap justification in Rev-1 §9

Our current spec uses HAC + bootstrap reconciliation for the T3b gate. DGK's headline inference (Figure 3, §4) uses a **50-day block bootstrap with 5000 replications** because the two-week returns overlap daily, and they state Hansen-Hodrick SEs are "not feasible" under their data structure (line 738). Our weekly Friday-anchored returns are *non-overlapping*, so Hansen-Hodrick is not forced on us, but a block bootstrap of comparable block length (say 12 weeks ≈ 60 trading days) would be methodologically aligned with DGK's published standard in a top-tier volatility-pricing paper. Recommend our HAC-bootstrap sensitivity row use a block length calibrated to DGK's practice rather than left unspecified.

### Action 3c (WEAK): Cite DGK as the canonical σ² vs. ε² distinction for our §2 LHS-choice discussion

Rev-1 currently defines TRM-RV as the LHS, cube-root-transformed. DGK §1.1 (lines 285–310) is a clean, citable articulation of why realized volatility (what we measure, ε²) is *not* the same as uncertainty (σ², a conditional variance). This matters because our exercise title says "TRM-RV" and a reviewer could conflate. A single sentence pointing to DGK fixes this.

### Action 3d (WEAK): Do NOT add a new sensitivity row from this paper

DGK's portfolio-construction method requires a liquid options surface on the underlying. **COP/USD has no such surface** (at best, thinly-traded NDF options offshore; onshore BVC options are essentially non-existent). Therefore we cannot replicate DGK's iv/rv decomposition for COP/USD even in principle, and no new sensitivity row is warranted. This is the tight upper bound on what DGK can contribute.

### Action 3e (none): No citation for §13 on remittance-surprise → FX-vol

DGK has no remittance or EM FX content; it cannot substitute for, nor complement, Basco & Ojeda-Joya 2024 Borrador 1273 as the Colombian-remittance-macro reference. The "thin §13 Colombian-remittance-surprise citation" gap is NOT closed by this paper.

## 4. Cautions and conflicts

### Caution 4a: Implied vs. realized volatility — our LHS is realized, but our gate interprets β̂ as if uncertainty-like

Our T3b tests whether remittance surprises *predict* TRM realized volatility. DGK §1.1 (lines 294–306) and §4.2 (lines 823–829) are explicit: realized volatility is a *backward-looking* quadratic function of returns, and both positive and negative uncertainty shocks induce higher RV. If a remittance surprise is interpreted as an "uncertainty shock" on COP/USD, DGK's framework says we should expect RV to rise on BOTH positive and negative surprises (magnitude, not sign). Our T3b is **two-sided on the coefficient**, not two-sided on the absolute value — which means a pre-registered sensitivity row on |remittance-surprise| → RV might be informative, *but* adding it now would violate our no-fishing protocol (memory: `project_fx_vol_econ_gate_verdict_and_product_read.md`). Flag as future-work only.

### Caution 4b: Rejecting pure GARCH(1,1) as the DGP

DGK footnote 12 (lines 317–318) and §3.2 (lines 605–614) document that for nonfinancials the average correlation between rv and iv innovations is 0.03 — "far from 1" — which they take as evidence *against* a pure GARCH(1,1) specification. **Our co-primary is GARCH(1,1)-X on the mean equation.** This is not a direct contradiction (we are estimating a mean-equation regressor's coefficient, not positing GARCH as the full DGP), but the Rev-4 reconciliation clause — "directional concordance between OLS and GARCH" — should note that GARCH(1,1) is known to understate idiosyncratic vol dynamics when implied-vol innovations carry independent information. For COP/USD, where no implied-vol series exists in our free-tier data, this caveat is academic. Recommend a brief footnote in Rev-1 §10 acknowledging this limitation cleanly.

### Caution 4c: No EM-FX or remittance identification in the paper

A reviewer who reads Rev-1 §13 expecting DGK to anchor remittance→FX-vol identification will find nothing. Do not oversell the citation; it is methodological color only.
